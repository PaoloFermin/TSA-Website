History

Heilig’s Sensorama Simulator, simulating riding a motorcycle through the city (Heimgartner, 2016).

Although augmented reality is seen as a new and emerging technology; the ability to overlay views into real life has been in the works for a little over 50 years. In the 1960s a man named Morton Heilig, “The father of virtual reality” made the first strides in mixing reality. A filmmaker and inventor, he invented the Sensorama Simulator in 1962. It stimulated all five senses to simulate riding a motorcycle down the streets of New York City. Six years later, Harvard professor Ivan Sutherland created the “ultimate display”, a wireframe room displayed through a head mounted binocular. It was required to be suspended from the ceiling above the user. This gained it the name, “The Sword of Damocles” as it had a similarly suspicious suspension system to the sword King Dionysius hung above Damocles while he sat on the king’s throne.

The next big leap in Augmented Reality came decades later in 1990, when Tom Caudell at Boeing coined the term “augmented reality” when working on a Head Mounted Display (HMD) for workers in airplane factories. The HMD allowed workers to see complex wiring diagrams projected on plane walls and assist workers in assembling them.

What finally brought augmented reality to the eyes of the people came 8 years later. It was comparatively simple in its method and information displayed but was seen widely and greatly appreciated. A method was developed for displaying the first down line on top of football games. This technique quickly spread to other programs including news, weather, and maps. Sometime around 2008 smartphone cameras began to be used in augmented reality apps. Then in 2013 one of the first mainstream HMDs was released by Google. Known as Google Glass it was a pair of glasses that had a screen projected on the lenses.
